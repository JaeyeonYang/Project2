Introduction:
The Charles Ahn Research Group focuses on the fabrication and characterization of novel complex oxide materials, aiming to unlock new functionalities for next-generation electronic devices. The lab employs advanced growth techniques such as molecular beam epitaxy and synchrotron x-ray scattering to produce high-quality thin films with atomically precise control. These sophisticated methods enable the exploration of multifunctional oxides—materials that can simultaneously exhibit magnetism, ferroelectricity, and superconductivity—paving the way for innovative device architectures.

A central focus of the group is the electronic control of complex order parameters, which is achieved through nanoscale manipulation using scanning probe microscopy and electronic writing. This research has direct implications for the development of nonvolatile logic switches, offering promising alternatives to traditional CMOS technology for post-CMOS and quantum computing. The group’s interdisciplinary approach, combining materials science, applied physics, and device engineering, ensures that fundamental discoveries are translated into practical technologies. Their work is at the forefront of the quantum revolution, shaping the future of electronics through the design and control of novel materials.

Professor Cao’s team bridges fundamental physics and engineering, employing advanced nanofabrication to create novel structures and high-resolution optical measurement to probe their properties. Their research spans the development of new light sources, coherent control of light in scattering media and multimode fibers, and the design of multifunctional photonic devices for biomedical imaging and beyond.

A hallmark of the lab’s methodology is its integration of experimental and computational approaches. Numerical modeling is heavily used to complement experiments, providing deep insights into the physical processes at play and enabling the prediction of new device behaviors. The group is also at the forefront of applying artificial intelligence to control chaos in optical systems, opening new avenues for manipulating light in complex environments. Applications range from speckle-free imaging and structured illumination microscopy to compact spectrometers based on disordered photonic chips. The lab’s interdisciplinary philosophy and commitment to both fundamental discovery and technological innovation have earned Professor Cao numerous prestigious honors, including election to the National Academy of Sciences and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.
